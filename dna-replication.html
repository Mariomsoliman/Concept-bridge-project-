Learn DNA Replication

What is DNA replication?

DNA replication is the process of making an exact copy of a DNA molecule before a cell divides. Each strand of the original double helix acts as a template for a new strand.

Why does it matter?

Without reliable replication, cells could lose or change important genetic instructions. Accurate copying keeps your traits, and the instructions inside your cells, consistent over time.

The double helix opens up so that each strand can serve as a template. An enzyme called helicase breaks the hydrogen bonds between the bases, separating the two strands like unzipping a zipper.

New free-floating nucleotides line up with the exposed bases following the simple matching rules: A pairs with T, and C pairs with G. This keeps the sequence of genetic information the same.

Enzymes connect the new nucleotides together, forming the sugar-phosphate backbone of each strand. In the end you have two DNA molecules, each with one original strand and one new strand.

Key enzymes involved

Important enzymes include helicase (unwinds the helix), primase (lays down a short starting piece), DNA polymerase (adds new nucleotides), and ligase (seals small gaps in the backbone).

Common errors and mutations

Occasionally, mistakes happen when DNA is copied. These changes are called mutations. Many are fixed or have no effect, but some can be harmful or, in rare cases, helpful for evolution.

Connection to Computer Science

DNA replication is a lot like copying data in computing. There is an input (the original DNA), an output (two copies), clear step-by-step rules, and built-in error checking. These ideas connect to topics like algorithms and time & space complexity in Computer Science.
